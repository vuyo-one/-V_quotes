OTHELLO

The Complete Quote Arsenal for NSC Grade 12

Iago Act 1, Sc 1
"I am not what I am."
Significance

The definitive paradox of the play. A biblical inversion of God's "I am that I am," establishing Iago as a satanic figure of pure deception. He warns us immediately: do not trust appearances.

Appearance vs Reality Biblical Allusion
Iago Act 3, Sc 3
"O, beware, my lord, of jealousy; It is the green-eyed monster which doth mock the meat it feeds on."
Significance

Iago uses reverse psychology, naming the emotion to plant it in Othello's mind. The metaphor suggests jealousy is a parasite that eats the host (Othello) from the inside out.

Jealousy Metaphor
Othello Act 3, Sc 3
"Haply, for I am black and have not those soft parts of conversation that chamberers have."
Significance

The turning point. Othello begins to internalize the racism he previously ignored. He views his race and lack of Venetian polish as flaws that would cause Desdemona to leave him.

Internalized Racism Insecurity
Othello Act 5, Sc 2
"Put out the light, and then put out the light."
Significance

Spoken before killing Desdemona. He equates blowing out a candle (literal light) with ending her life (spiritual light). It shows he sees this as a ritual sacrifice, not a murder.

Symbolism Tragic Irony
Iago Act 5, Sc 2
"Demand me nothing. What you know, you know. From this time forth I never will speak word."
Significance

Iago's final act of control. By refusing to explain his motives, he denies everyone closure. This reinforces Coleridge's idea of "motiveless malignity."

Power Ambiguity
Iago Act 1, Sc 1
"Even now, now, very now, an old black ram is tupping your white ewe."
Significance

Uses crude animal imagery to dehumanize Othello and panic Brabantio. The contrast of black/white and ram/ewe reduces their love to bestial lust.

Racism Imagery

Iago Act 2, Sc 1
"With as little a web as this will I ensnare as great a fly as Cassio."
Significance

Spider imagery. Iago watches Cassio kiss Desdemona's hand and plans to twist this innocent courtesy into a fatal trap. Shows his opportunistic nature.

Predator Imagery Plotting
Iago Act 3, Sc 3
"Trifles light as air are to the jealous confirmations strong as proofs of holy writ."
Significance

Explains the psychology of the handkerchief plot. To a jealous mind, meaningless things (trifles) become absolute gospel truth (holy writ).

Psychology The Handkerchief
Emilia Act 3, Sc 4
"They are all but stomachs, and we all but food; they eat us hungerly, and when they are full, they belch us."
Significance

A cynical view of men. Emilia explains that men use women for pleasure/utility and discard them. Contrasts with Desdemona's idealistic view of Othello.

Gender Roles Metaphor
Othello Act 3, Sc 3
"Excellent wretch! Perdition catch my soul, but I do love thee! And when I love thee not, chaos is come again."
Significance

Tragic foreshadowing. Othello admits that without Desdemona, his world will lose all order (chaos). This literally happens: as his love dies, his rationality collapses.

Foreshadowing Love
Iago Act 1, Sc 3
"Virtue! a fig! 'tis in ourselves that we are thus or thus. Our bodies are our gardens, to the which our wills are gardeners."
Significance

Iago's manifesto. He rejects fate and emotion, believing in absolute self-control and will. He "gardens" his own personality to manipulate others.

Free Will Rationalism
Desdemona Act 4, Sc 2
"His unkindness may defeat my life, but never taint my love."
Significance

Highlighting her tragic loyalty. Even as Othello abuses her, she remains steadfast. This can be seen as ultimate virtue or tragic passivity.

Victimhood Loyalty
Othello Act 5, Sc 2
"I took by the throat the circumcised dog, and smote him thus."
Significance

His final act. He identifies himself with the "Turk" (the enemy of Venice) and kills himself to punish the enemy within. A complex moment of reclaiming honor through suicide.

Redemption Outsider
Iago Act 1, Sc 1
"Were I the Moor I would not be Iago. In following him I follow but myself."
Significance

A clear admission of self-interest. He follows Othello only to serve his own ends. It establishes the theme that Iago's loyalty is a performance.

Machiavellian Deceit
Othello Act 5, Sc 2
"That handkerchief which I so loved and gave thee thou gav'st to Cassio."
Significance

The climax of the symbol. The handkerchief, once a symbol of love, has become a symbol of infidelity. Othello is so blinded by "evidence" he ignores her denial.

The Handkerchief Blindness
Emilia Act 5, Sc 2
"You told a lie, an odious, damned lie! Upon my soul, a lie, a wicked lie!"
Significance

Emilia's moral awakening. She defies her husband and societal expectations of silence to speak the truth, ultimately sacrificing her life for it.
